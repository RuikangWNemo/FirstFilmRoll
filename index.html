时光印记

巴城古街的石板路与斑驳古门，存留着岁月低语；我首次用胶卷捕捉那一瞬间的悸动，便将往昔定格于光影之间。

巴城古街的低语

位于昆山腹地的巴城古街，青石板路、古槐林立，每一块石板都在静静诉说着流逝的岁月与人情冷暖。这里保存着古城的历史记忆与市井往事，见证了时光的沉淀。

那一天，我怀着既忐忑又激动的心情首次握住黑白胶卷相机。按下快门的瞬间，仿佛听见老街上时光轻语，那一刻，光影成为永恒。

艺术家陈述

艺术家陈述

对我来说，摄影是一种放慢脚步去真正观察的方式。这卷黑白胶卷是我第一次接触胶片摄影。在拍摄的过程中，我不只是学习如何使用一台相机，我也在学习如何去“看”、如何去“等待”、以及如何去相信那些不确定性的过程。

在这之前，我习惯使用无反数码相机拍照。拍摄时按下快门几乎是本能动作，我常常在一次外拍中拍摄上千张照片。比如，在杜克大学校园附近的 Lakewood 小学做纪录项目时，我曾在两小时内拍下了超过 2700 张照片。虽然最后挑选出的照片效果不错，但我那时相信“量变产生质变”，拍得越多，选出来的好照片就越多。

而胶卷摄影完全改变了我的这种思维方式。每一张照片都成了一种抉择。我不得不停下来，更深入地观察场景与被摄对象，去思考拍摄的意义与方式。手动调整快门速度与光圈的过程，也让我第一次真正意识到摄影的“机械性”与“感知性”之间的联系。通过这卷胶卷，我开始理解到，摄影不仅关乎拍什么，也关乎你是以怎样的状态去拍的。

我被摄影中那些微妙、易逝的瞬间所吸引，比如空间的质感、双手的节奏、日常中被忽略的诗意。这些照片并不只是对现实的记录，更是关于“在场”的实践，是一种存在于当下的姿态。在拍摄中，我试图不过度安排、不急于构图，而是选择留白与等待，让场景自然展开。

这第一卷胶卷让我明白，有意义的影像并不始于相机，而是始于我们的眼睛、身体，以及我们对周围世界的回应与倾听。

第一卷胶卷的故事：巴城、三轮车与共同完成的旅程

这组照片拍摄于中国江苏昆山的一个古镇——巴城。那天，我受我的导师 Kaley Clements 教授的邀请，参加了一场摄影工作坊，由 Laurids Sonne 教授组织并主持。在巴城的街巷中，我也与 Charles Watson 教授同行，他鼓励我从更深的层次思考一张照片的意义——不仅仅是画面好不好看，而是它背后是否有故事与价值。在旅程的最后阶段，Cici Cheng 教授与 Tom Rankin 教授也加入了我们。

而真正的冒险，也从那一刻开始。

Tom Rankin 教授五年前曾来过昆山，他这次回来，想重走当年的路径。他带来了大画幅相机和一辆三轮车，想用它穿行于巴城的街道，记录人们的日常生活。这个江南水乡布满老墙、青瓦、小巷与河埠头，老工人坐在门口，洗衣的、卖菜的、晾衣服的……一切都带着未被打扰的生活质感，非常适合去捕捉那些真实的片刻。

但问题是，三轮车的座位太矮了。

于是，我们开始了一段即兴维修的“任务”。我们先找到一家小焊接铺子，让师傅帮我们接了一截同样直径的钢管来加长坐杆。但这个钢管太薄，稳固性不够。接着我们又在街上转了几圈，发现了一家竹编铺。里面的手艺人非常热情，帮我们找了一根合适的木棍插进钢管中，以增强支撑力。他细心地打磨、安装，最后却坚持不收一分钱。我们便送了他一张我从杜克换来的五美元纸币作为纪念。他拿着那张纸币开心地笑了，像是收到了一份礼物，Tom 也跟着笑了。这是一个很安静、但令人难忘的瞬间。

修好的三轮车，成了那一天的“摄影工具”，也成了我们故事的载体。我们慢慢地穿过小镇，看光线在砖墙和屋檐间移动，观察人们在日常中穿行。我不再想着要拍出“完美”的构图，而是想着如何真正地“在那儿”——观察、等待，并与这个地方产生真实的连接。

临走前，Tom 从口袋里拿出一张二十美元纸币递给我，说：“拿着它，去买点和摄影有关的东西。”我接过来看了一眼，笑着回答说：“这不仅是二十美元，这是一件艺术品，是个‘杰作’。”我们都笑了。那个时刻，我看到了他的慷慨，也感受到了他对纪录与艺术的真诚热爱。他不是在“给钱”，他是在传递一种精神。

这些照片没有摆拍，也没有刻意安排。它们是生活碎片的拼接，是一整天自然而然的流动，是即兴创作与人际联结共同作用下的成果。

这，是我的第一卷胶卷。但更重要的是，这是我第一次真正用影像去记录一段信任、一种学习、一种“慢下来并去看见”的共同体验。

Artist Statement

Artist Statement

Photography, to me, is about slowing down enough to truly notice. With this first roll of black-and-white film, I wasn’t just learning how to use a camera. I was learning how to see, how to wait, and how to trust the process of uncertainty.

Before this project, I was used to shooting with mirrorless digital cameras. It was normal for me to take hundreds or even thousands of images in a short span of time. For instance, during a documentary project at Lakewood Elementary School near Duke University, I took over 2,700 photos in two hours. The final selections turned out well, but my process was rooted in abundance rather than intention. I thought quantity could lead to quality.

Shooting on film challenged that mindset completely. Every frame became a decision. I had to pause, observe, and engage more deeply with the subject and the environment. Adjusting shutter speed and aperture manually required me to understand the mechanics of photography, not just rely on instinct. Through this process, I began to appreciate not only what I was capturing, but how and why I was doing it.

I am drawn to photography as a way of recording quiet, fleeting gestures. I try to capture the texture of lived spaces, the rhythm of working hands, and the poetry found in ordinary routines. These images are not simply about documentation. They are about presence. About being fully there in the moment, without rushing or over-composing, and allowing the scene to breathe on its own.

This first roll taught me that meaningful photography does not begin with the camera. It begins with how you see, how you move, and how you choose to listen to your surroundings.

The Story of the First Roll: Bacheng, the Tricycle, and a Shared Journey

This series was photographed in Bacheng, a centuries-old water town in Kunshan, China. I was invited by my mentor, Professor Kaley Clements, to join a photography workshop organized by Professor Laurids Sonne. During the journey, I was also guided by Professor Charles Watson, who encouraged me to think more carefully about what makes an image meaningful. He helped me consider not just the aesthetic appeal of an image, but the story and human value behind it. Later, Professors Cici Cheng and Tom Rankin joined us for the final part of the trip.

That is when the real adventure began.

Professor Tom Rankin had visited DKU and Kunshan five years earlier, and he wanted to revisit Bacheng in the same way as before. He brought along a tricycle to carry his large-format camera and planned to photograph the everyday life of the town. The alleys and canals of Bacheng are filled with timeless textures—crumbling brick walls, hanging laundry, shopkeepers sitting on stools, and the natural choreography of daily life in Jiangnan. It was a perfect setting to reflect on what documentary photography can be.

But there was a problem. The tricycle’s seat was too short.

So, Tom and I set off on an impromptu mission to solve it. First, we found a local welder who extended the seat post using a steel pipe. But the new pipe was too thin to be stable. We needed reinforcement. After wandering through narrow streets, we found a bamboo weaving shop. Inside, an artisan enthusiastically helped us find a wooden rod to insert into the pipe for extra strength. He worked with great care, hand-carving and fitting the piece. In the end, he refused to take any money. Instead, we offered him a small souvenir: a five-dollar bill I had exchanged back at Duke. He smiled, held the bill up like a prize, and laughed. Tom smiled too. It was a simple moment of human connection that I will never forget.

That repaired tricycle became the vehicle, both literally and metaphorically, for the photos I took that day. We wandered slowly through the town, watching the light shift across bricks and rooftops, capturing glimpses of people living their lives. I was not thinking about perfection. I was thinking about presence, about witnessing, and about sharing a moment with a place and its people.

Before Tom left Kunshan, he reached into his pocket and handed me a folded twenty-dollar bill. He told me to use it to get something more for photography. I looked at it, paused, and said, "This isn’t just twenty dollars. This is a piece of art. A masterpiece." We both laughed. In that short exchange, I felt his kindness, his spirit, and his genuine love for both documentary and art. He was not just giving money. He was passing along something deeper.

These photographs are not staged or arranged. They are fragments of real life, gathered in the natural flow of a day that blended documentation, collaboration, and quiet improvisation.

This is my first roll of film. But more than that, it is a record of trust, shared learning, and what becomes possible when you slow down and truly pay attention.
